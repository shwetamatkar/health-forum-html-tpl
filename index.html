The Coronavirus Mental Health Crisis and its Psychological impact

Radhika Agarwal

June 10, 2020

We’ve spent about a 100 days in fear and made dramatic changes to how we live. We are quarantined for weeks, scared of catching COVID-19, cities are in lock-down, we might lose our jobs, and we cannot see how this ends. Uncertainty about what the future holds, the isolation from loved ones, the restrictions on leisure, the disruption of old habits and routines, the newly developed fears and worries, etc. test our psychological health and resilience. Our well-being has been whammed, probably like never before. Because there is a rise in interest in coronavirus and mental health, I’ll highlight what we have learned from psychological studies conducted on the impact of the CoVID-19 pandemic.

The COVID-19 pandemic is not just a public health crisis for the duration of the disease, it is also a mental health crisis and people’s psychological well-being needs to be addressed. Calls to mental health hotlines have dramatically risen for a variety of reasons like suicide ideation and addiction. Telehealth options like phone/chat/video counseling, self-help forums, mental health apps may be our only option in seeking professional help; and fortunately, these methods are effective in dealing with stress, addictions, loneliness, fear, grief, anxiety, PTSD, suicide, and depression. A new form of therapy called structured letter therapy (involves writing a document with questions and pointers) is already recommended as an alternative during quarantine.

What should one know about mental health?

Aman Mathur

June 10, 2020

I am a teenager who has recently started suffering from severe depression. Well actually I wish it was that simple, but it has been a downhill slope over the past few years that has lead to things such as suicidal ideation (contemplating the value of your life, and the idea of suicide). I live in a developed country (Canada), so getting help really is not that difficult, but what makes it bad is the stigma against mental health, which still exists here in. There is also the fact that most of the population has a limited understanding of mental disorders, so when they try to help, they may actually make it worse. Let me give you a few examples that apply to depression:

“Just snap out of it!” Not that simple, considering this is an actual illness, like cancer, or the flu, except it lasts much longer, and is absolutely crippling.

“It’s all in your head” Yeah, thanks. People with depression already think everything is their fault, so telling them this will deter them from seeking treatment.

“You just need to look at the bright side, be more optimistic” I can’t control it, the little voice in my head just constantly tells me I am worthless and have no use. I honestly have no control of my emotions, that is why depression is classified as a mood disorder.

Mental illnesses affect a large portion of the population, so we must teach the public about them, just like we learn about the basic human anatomy. When society becomes more open to those with mental illnesses, and when we learn to help those who suffer from these illnesses, people become more willing to seek help.

What is ideal mental health?

Siddhesh Kandarkar

June 09, 2020

That’s an incredibly general question and I would imagine many people might have different answers. In my opinion, good mental health contains several parts. One piece of the puzzle is having a high level of self-awareness enabling one to step out of stressful situations and take time for self-care. Being in tune with your self or being self-aware enables you to know what self-care you need to do to keep yourself emotionally healthy. The next piece of the puzzle is having a healthy routine and a self-care regimen that you know is part of keeping you emotionally healthy (or mentally healthy). There are a variety of important pieces to one’s self-care that are important, including eating healthily, exercising regularly, meditating, socializing the right amount, sleeping the right amount, and doing some kind of art and/or spiritual growth. That brings me to my third important activity which is developing one’s life purpose. This often involves a period of spiritual growth and self-awareness to be able to figure out the path that brings your heart joy. This can also be a path to empathy, curiosity, and humility, all crucial characteristics of an emotionally/mentally healthy human being. Why is mental health so taboo?

Jet Paterson

June 09, 2020

For centuries many illnesses, including mental illness, were viewed as a scourge visited on people who had done some wrong/sin, or whose families had sinned. Everything from the black plague to HIV, even cancer, were once touted as divine punishment for some moral flaw. It was with knowledge of the cause of these diseases that their stigma disappeared. Knowledge has generally tended to remove blame and fear wherever it shines its light.

Mental illnesses retain such a harsh stigma because, unlike many other diseases, we still don't *understand* them very well on a biological/medical basis. And as humans, we tend to fear the unknown.

Unfortunately, the brain has remained a "black box" to scientists for years, because it is just so hard to study. As technology improves, the brian becomes accessible, and we learn more and more; most of the advances that have been made in psychiatry have been made just since the 1980's! And as we learn more, we understand that mental illness DOES have organic causes the way that cancer or heart disease does.

As we begin to identify biological/chemical changes that bring about mental illness, to find pharmacologic treatments for them, and as it becomes popular knowledge that mental illness has a physiological root, I believe the blame and the fear will dissipate. People will recognize that these are not moral defects or punishment or some inherent "weirdness"...they are medical conditions that are not earned/deserved, and that can be treated the way an infection can be treated with antibiotics
